Teenagers, Technology, and Social Media: The Shifting Landscape of Digital Connection in 2024

Social media and content creation have become integral to modern life, especially for Gen Z, the first generation raised in a fully digital world. This analysis explores how teenagers are engaging with these platforms today, highlighting the dominant players and evolving usage trends.

Connected more than ever

According to study by Pew Research Center survey of U.S., most teens use social media and have a smartphone and nearly half say they are online almost constantly. This number had nearly doubled since Pew released this survey in 2015.

Among the social media giants, we see that Youtube has been a dominating player among them. 9 out of 10 teens report using the site. This number is slightly down from the 95% in 2022.

Furthermore, roughly 6 in 10 teen report using other platforms such as Tiktok and Instagram, while only about 55% use Snapchat.

What is the impact of social media on teens and young adults?

In an article by etactics, Maria Clark highlights the rising mental health concerns among younger generations. A 2017 study of students in grades 8 through 12 found that between 2010 and 2015, high levels of depressive symptoms increased by 33%. During the same period, the suicide rate among girls in this age group rose by 65%.

Furthermore, a report by The Sun in 2020 that revealed that child suicide rates had risen by as much as 150%, while self-harm among girls aged 10 to 14 had nearly tripled. Experts suggest that these troubling trends are largely linked to the rise of social media.

Does reduced screen time mean more positive emotions?

A 2023 study by Pew Research found that a majority of teens report experiencing positive emotions when they are without their phones. Specifically, 74% of teens say they at least sometimes feel happy, and 72% report feeling peaceful when disconnected. In fact, net positive emotions (happiness and peace) are nearly twice as common as negative emotions (anxiety, loneliness, and distress). While smartphones remain an integral part of teenage life, this study suggests that many young people still find a sense of relief and well-being when taking a break from their screens.

Conclusion

After a decade or more after social media had become an integral part of society, we have enough evidence and research to understand the potential consequences of too much screen time and too much social media. While the platforms themselves are not inherently bad, the way they are used and the amount of time spent on them can have a significant impact on mental health and well-being. It is essential for parents, educators, and policymakers to be aware of these trends and to take steps to mitigate the negative effects of social media on young people. By promoting healthy screen time habits and encouraging face-to-face interactions, we can help ensure that the next generation grows up in a balanced and supportive environment.
